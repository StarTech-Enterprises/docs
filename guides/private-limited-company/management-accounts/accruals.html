    Accruals

    Guides • Private Limited Company • 15/11/2019

    Overview

    These management accounts try to make the task of calculating any Accrued Revenue or Accrued Expenses as hassle free as possible. This is what the Accruals tab is for.

    Although you should not really have to do anything in this tab, the tab is visible so you can see exactly how the Accrual calculations are being made. Below are some screen shots:

    As you can see above, there is one Mobile Phone bill that was invoiced in arrears (received some time in March), with a net figure of £41.67. The expense was for the provision of the service that started in the middle of February and ended in the middle of March. This is why, in the “Type” column: ‘Accrued Expense’ is chosen.

    Given the above conditions, the columns further on in the sheet ensure that the expense gets correctly spread over the two months. Other columns also ensure that the Accrued Expense balance shows up at the end of February. See below:

    As shown above, the expense of £41.67 gets spread over both February (£32.26) and March (£9.41), as it should.

    A small Accrued Expense balance (of £32.26) also gets recognised in February, as it should.

    That is really all there is to this tab. The only thing you have to be careful of is to ensure that the Table doesn’t run out of rows, as the number of transactions in the workbook grows. Otherwise just drag and expand the rows in the Table accordingly.

    Formulae used

    Accrued Revenue / Expense

    The following logic is used to calculate the Accrued Revenue / Expense Charge to the Profit & Loss. It can easily be customised for your own specific accounting rule logic, if they depart from the normal rules:

    MS Excel 
    = IFERROR(LET(
    start_date,Table_Accruals[@[Start Date]:[Start Date]],end_date,Table_Accruals[@[End Date]:[End Date]],days, MAX(end_date-start_date+1, 0),date_array,SEQUENCE(1,days,start_date,1),amount,Table_Accruals[@[Net]:[Net]],
    start_date_month,Y$16,end_date_month,DATE(YEAR(start_date_month),MONTH(start_date_month)+1,0),
    date_matrix,(TRANSPOSE(date_array)>=start_date_month)*(TRANSPOSE(date_array)<=end_date_month), days_in_month, TRANSPOSE(MMULT(TRANSPOSE(date_matrix),SEQUENCE(ROWS(date_matrix),1,1,0))),
    pnl_amount,days_in_month/days*amount,
    full_amount, AND(Table_Accruals[@[Invoice Date]:[Invoice Date]]>EOMONTH(start_date_month,-1),Table_Accruals[@[Invoice Date]:[Invoice Date]]<=EOMONTH(start_date_month,0)),
    acc_def_test, OR(Table_Accruals[@[Type]:[Type]]="Acc/Def Revenue",Table_Accruals[@[Type]:[Type]]="Acc/Def Expense"),
    pnl_result, SWITCH(TRUE(), acc_def_test, pnl_amount, full_amount, Table_Accruals[@[Net]:[Net]], 0), pnl_result), "")

    Accrued Balance

    The following is used to calculate the Accrued Revenue / Expense Balance for the Balance Sheet. This can also be customised for your own specific accounting rule logic, if they depart from the normal rules:

    MS Excel 
    = IFERROR(LET(
    amount,Table_Accruals[@[Net]:[Net]],pnl,Table_Accruals[@[JAN P&L]:[DEC P&L]], due_date, Table_Accruals[@[Due Date]:[Due Date]],
    start_month, $AK$16, start_date_month, AK$16, end_date_month,DATE(YEAR(start_date_month),MONTH(start_date_month)+1,0), start_date_months, EOMONTH(start_month, SEQUENCE(1,12,0,1) - 1) + 1,
    start_date, Table_Accruals[@[Start Date]:[Start Date]], end_date, Table_Accruals[@[End Date]:[End Date]],
    date_matrix,TRANSPOSE(start_date_months)<=start_date_month, amount_matrix, TRANSPOSE(pnl)*date_matrix,
    prior_year_amount, IF(AND(start_date < start_month, end_date >= start_month), (start_month - 1 - start_date + 1)/(end_date - start_date + 1) * amount, 0),
    accrued_amount, TRANSPOSE(MMULT(TRANSPOSE(amount_matrix),SEQUENCE(ROWS(amount_matrix),1,1,0))) + prior_year_amount, cleared_out_result, IF(due_date < end_date_month + 1, 0, accrued_amount),
    accrued_test, OR(Table_Accruals[@[Type]:[Type]]="Acc/Def Revenue",Table_Accruals[@[Type]:[Type]]="Acc/Def Expense"),
    accrued_BS, SWITCH(TRUE(), accrued_test, cleared_out_result, 0), accrued_BS), "")

    Accrual Scenarios

    Accrue retrospectively

    You may have noticed that the Accruals here, with the method used so far, only get retrospectively calculated and recorded to the correct historical dates - that is on the date when the relevant invoice has been received.

    So, for example, if in the middle of Month 3, you get an invoice of £100, for services that started in the middle of Month 1 and ended in the middle of Month 2, you would record the invoice, only when you get it, in the middle of Month 3, as follows:

    In Month 3 tab:

    Field Value
    Invoice Date 15/03/2020
    Tax Point Date 15/03/2020
    Start Date 15/01/2020
    End Date 15/02/2020
    Transaction P/L Invoice (or S/L Invoice)
    Due Date 15/03/2020
    Ledger P/L (or S/L)
    Invoice Amount £100
    Type Acc/Def Expense (or Acc/Def Revenue)

    Important: Just before getting the invoice, in Month 3, nothing would have been booked!

    98% of the time, this retrospective recognition of accurals is perfectly acceptable for these types of accounts, since in the examples shown, the invoices are raised quite frequently, for services that cover relatively short time periods (e.g. a month). So retrospective bookings, when made frequently over the course of the year won’t result in any inaccuracies (except, perhaps, in the very last month, in December.)

    Accrue on the go

    If, at the end of Month 1, you would really like to record an Accrued Expense, (or Accrued Revenue) for the Invoice that you expect to get much later, in the middle of Month 3, then you would instead do this, when accounting for the transactions in the workbook.

    New Revenue Rules

    Important:
    IFRS 15 vs IAS 18

    In 2018, there was a slight change to the accounting recognition of revenue (these rules don’t apply to expenses)

    IFRS 15 replaced IAS 18, as the main revenue recognition standard. The standard has more complex rules around when revenue can be recongised. Performance obligations to a contract have to be separately identified, and unlike IAS 18, any contract liability (deferred revenue) and trade receivable need to be shown net until the earlier of either:

    • The date the payment becomes due (i.e. when the ‘receivable’ is recognised), or
    • The date the goods or services are delivered (i.e. when a ‘contract asset’ (accrued revenue) is recognised).

    Under IFRS 15, payment, for non-cancellable contracts, payment becomes due on the date at which the cash payment should be received under the contract, not the invoice date of the trade receivable.

    Under IAS 18, deferred revenue and trade receivables could be recognised ‘Gross’ on the balance sheet. Under IFRS 15, they need to be recognised ‘Net’

    Example

    If as a company, you invoice in full at the start (say 1 January 2020), for a 2 year maintenance service contract, totalling £24,000, which is payable in full on 31 March 2020, you would make the following entries:

    IAS 18

    Under IAS 18, the following entries would be made:

    1 January:
    Dr Receviable £24,000
    Cr Deferred Revenue £24,000

    31 January:
    Dr Deferred Revenue £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    28 February:
    Dr Deferred Revenue £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    31 March:
    Dr Deferred Revenue £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    IFRS 15

    Under IFRS 15, the following entries would be made:

    1 January:
    Dr -
    Cr -

    31 January:
    Dr Contract Asset (Accrued Revenue) £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    28 February:
    Dr Contract Asset (Accrued Revenue) £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    31 March:
    Dr Contract Asset (Accrued Revenue) £1,000
    Cr Revenue £1,000

    Dr Receivable £24,000
    Cr Contract Asset (Accrued Revenue) £3,000
    Cr Contract Liability (Deferred Revenue) £21,000

    Important:
    The example was taken from here: BDO IFRS 15 - Revenue Example

    How do the New Rules affect this workbook?

    Fortunately, the rule change does not affect the workbook and its calculations too much. Sales Invoices are usually raised in arrears, after the work is done (the example above is for a Sales Invoice raised in advance). There is also the option, if needed, to record any accruals over each month, on the go, until such time the invoice is issued, instead of relying on retrospectively recording accruals.

    Cash Due Date vs Invoice Date:

    The only small difference from the old standard is that the Trade Receivable should, according to our reading of the new rules, be recognised when the cash for the invoice is due, rather than on the invoice date (for non-cancellable contracts).

    So, if a sales invoice was raised on 15/03/2020, and was due for payment on 22/03/2020 (c. 7 days later) any Trade Receivable on the Balance Sheet ought to be recognised on 22/03/2020, (and any Accrued Revenue, if there is any, reversed on that date), instead of on 15/03/2020, the Invoice Date.

    Accordingly, therefore, the “Due Date” field should be set to 22/03/2020.

    VAT due on Tax Point Date:

    However, according to other rules, the VAT payable or recoverable is still based on the Tax Point Date, which in this case is 15/03/2020 (the earlier of the Invoice Date and Date of Supply upto when the Invoice Date is more than 14 days after the Date of Supply - otherwise the Tax Point Date becomes the Date of Supply).

    Interpretation:

    Therefore, if the Tax Point date is earlier than the Due Date, the following would be recognised

    Dr Receivable (VAT Amount)
    Cr VAT Payable (VAT Amount)

    This is what we believe should happen. The VAT payable should be recognised on the Tax Point Date - and the other side to Receivables, even though the full receivable won’t really be recognised until the Invoice Due Date (in accordnace with IFRS 15).

    So, overall, it looks like we are still ok, with the new rules.
