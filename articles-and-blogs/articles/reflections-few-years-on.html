Reflections a few years on - 26 Questions!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 13/10/2017

Inspired by the thoughts and views of people I respect and greatly admire - I spent some time this week reflecting on the past few years, the next few years, and where life’s next new exciting adventures might take me!

Written in the form of a series of interviewer and interviewee questions, I start with that great question - “life achievements”!

Question 1

Q1. Some people might say you have achieved a lot in your life so far?

A1. It’s always nice to be told that, but I think many people have achieved many things, and that what you achieve and how you see those achievements, depend a lot on your circumstances, your situation, and own perspective. I think what’s important to consider are not just your tangible achievements, but the many other intangible things you achieve too. Moreover, it may be that you achieve one thing very well, rather than many things to a mediocre level. So when it comes to thinking about people’s achievements, there are many things to consider!

Question 2

Q2. You performed quite well when you were young at school. Were you always top of your class?

A2. Certainly not. Through my school years, I was very strong at Maths and the Sciences, but relatively weak, for example, in some of the Humanities and Arts subjects, such as English Literature, History, and The Classics.

But, now “Leadership” and “Communication” are things I have become more and more passionate about, so I am not “as strong” on the mathematical and technical dimensions as I used to be. Over recent years, I’ve just had much more of a passion for speaking and communicating. It is something I feel I needed to work more on.

Question 3

Q3. What are the key skills you took away from your schooling days?

A3. For me, it wasn’t so much about what I learnt from subjects taught in the classroom - though that was obviously important. But really what I learnt from my friends, and classmates - because those are things, I think, stay with you forever!

At school, when I was really young, I didn’t really play that many sports outside my regular PE lessons; I knew I was a very studious boy - something that was strongly shaped by my parents and the educational system I was brought up in. But then, after a few years, in the breaks and lunchtimes, I got more and more into playing sports, such as basketball, tennis, cricket, and football…, just to try out new things.

So I think, one of the most important lessons for me, wasn’t any kind of curriculum lesson, but a life lesson – to not be afraid of trying out new things! I think one should always try to push themselves outside of their comfort zones, and not be afraid of failure, because “failure” , is just part of learning process. If you’re not failing, you’re not learning! So, when you start something new, you just have to realise that you won’t be as good as other people – but you will, with time, get better!.

I think, as a phrase related to this, Aristotle once said “We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit” - so practice here definitely helps!

So try something new! Some things you will fail at AND won’t like! That’s fine! But there will be other things that you do like. You might fail in these at the start, but because you are so passionate about these things, - you like them so much - you’re just going to have the guts to continue doing that - to continue pursuing your passions!

A few years into my career, for example, I choose to leave a well-paid professional job to pursue an MBA. One of the things that I felt I needed to work really hard on, at the time, were my leadership and communication skills. I’m sure some people might have thought, why am I doing an MBA, and why in the UK, as a native English speaker? Am I doing this because I want to try to humble myself? But this was absolutely not the case!

I had many reasons and objectives. One of these was from a personal desire to want to improve and develop my Leadership and Communication skills, and so I was willing and prepared to work hard on these things, despite the high opportunity costs!

Question 4

Q4. If you had the power, how would you change our educational system?

A4. I think what I would change is not so much in terms of the subject matter or the various topics that are taught, but the style of teaching. I feel a lot of education around the world, today at least, is done too much by rote - you’re just tested and rewarded by how well you can just memorise facts. I think some of this is imporant, and necessary, to show that you possess certain knowledge. In certain taught subjects, though, I think the rote learning can be excessive. I found this, for example, the case in certain subjects at school in subjects like: history and geography, and true to a large extent, when at the start of my career, I pursued my Chartered Qualification in Finance & Accounting - there is a lot to rote learn!

The obvious big drawback with rote learning is that you don’t get taught “how to think”! You just memorise facts. So, I think, just teaching people how to think, both practically and logically, combined with some emotional and situational awareness, rather than just learning how to recite facts, is a very important life skill!

Question 5

Q5. What are we missing most as a society?

A5. I think one important thing, is the pursuit of the long (rather than the short) term. I think with many people these days, work and life is becoming more and more about wanting to get something immediately. We have iPhones, we have iPads, we have Laptops, - instant gratification is available at our finger tips. And if somebody is bored, say in a restaurant meal, and don’t find that conversation interesting, they’ll immediately be on SnapChat or on their phones. It has become an unhealthy obsession!

Along with the pursuit of the long term, I think just being patient is also very important, and something that is sometimes missing from society! For example, when working on homework problems at your desk, are you focused more on your homework, or are you just being distracted by your iPhone the whole time?

And this kind of mindset and behaviour can apply to many other things too. For example, one way to get slim is just to have liposuction. But a better way would be to just change your diet and exercise habits - because that’s going to have much longer-term benefits!

Now, we live in a world and society surrounded by a lot of these “new age technologies”. Whilst I’ll admit that some of these technologies can make certain aspects of our lives easy, if they are always going to make people 24/7 hellbent on wanting to get and find INSTANT gratification, and INSTANT solutions, then I think people are not going to be engaged enough in investing the time in their own personal development.

Question 6

Q6. Do you think this is going to be a problem with technology, causing us to be focusing on things we are not meant to be focusing on?

A6. Absolutely! When I did my MBA, I sometimes found when a Professor gave his or her lecture, that when an MBA student raised their hand and asked a question, other people would sometimes be on their iPhone or laptop, just thinking, “oh, this person doesn’t really understand anything”.

Now, I think, if you do not RESPECT that person’s question, it is YOU who loses out! - because if you cannot respect some of the qualities in another person, then it is your ignorance for not being able to see that!

Anyone who comes to a business school programme deserves to be there! They will each have there own reasons, and if just completely ignore that person, then that is YOUR prejudice for not listening to that person.

Question 7

Q7. You studied Engineering at University, but then, when you graduated, started your career in the professional services industry. Why did you decide to next pursue this path?

A7. Call it creative exploration!

I think, after studying science & engineering for several years, and then doing various Technology & Engineering Internships, what I was really wanted, was to learn and get was a different perspective into business, something different from the research and development experiences I had gained, mostly in reseach labs, till then.

When I looked back at what my Engineering degree gave me, I saw that it had cultivated an interest and given me a good understanding (both technical and managerial) of “technology” and “manufacturing” - things I don’t think I would have had the opportunity to learn about without my degree!

Then, when I reflected on the various work experiences I did over my University summers, I thought about the summer internship I did with PwC in my penultimate year, in London. I quite liked the work I did. I quite liked the people I worked with. And I quite liked what the department within the company did. I also always felt, in the back of my mind, that getting a few years of work experience for a company such PwC, would provide an “excellent and rigorous apprenticeship”, and “very strong foundation”, for a career in any kind of business, later down the line.

So with these positives in mind, after graduating from University, I decided to start my post graduate career with PwC.

Now, that’s not to say that I didn’t think I would want to use the skills, knowledge, and networks I would gain, to say, later work for an innovative Engineering, Media or Technology company - one whose products and services I felt really excited about – later on in my career. Both my grandfather and father were Engineers for much of their careers, before they later moved into more commercial, general management, and leadership type positions in their respective companies and industries.

I felt it would be a huge honour and priviledge to be able to follow in their footsteps, and continue the family legacy in some way, by eventually moving back into Technology or Engineering. The path I chose early on in my career, just happened to be with PwC.

Question 8

Q8. What were your experiences like at PwC?

A8. I learnt a tremendous amount. Overall, I had a decent time.

It was certainly a time that made me realise the tremendous impact that those working in Professional Services can have on society. When I was there:

  • I got to work with, and learn from a lot of smart, motivated, and very experienced people - from a range of different ethnic backgrounds, cultures and ages – but all with a very keen interest in business, and in what it takes to run, grow, develop, or acquire one.
  • I also got the opportunity to work with a wide range of companies, to help solve a range of financial problems - which was extremely stimulating!

Question 9

Q9. Can you tell me a bit more about PwC, and when you were there the path you took?

A9. Well, you can think of most Professional Service companies (or firms as they like to call themselves), as having three main areas:

(i) Tax, (ii) Business Consulting, and (iii) Business Assurance.

For the first and last, I would say you need very strong Finance & Accounting and Organisational skills; and for Business Consulting, you need strong Creative, Analytical, and Logical thinking skills. In my case, my path started in the Transaction Advisory department - in a small division called the Strategy Group, where I worked as a General Business Analyst - doing mostly desk-based market research - e.g. Commercial Due Dilligence work. I worked in that division, in a full time position, for 1.5 years.

Then, after working in the company’s Transaction Advisory department, for about 18 months, I started to become more and more interested in the idea of pursuing a Chartered Qualification, something like a C.A. - which the company offered, and encouraged many of its Employees to do.

As a technical qualification, my thoughts at the time – particularly straight after doing something like an Engineering degree – but also given the economic slowdown that was happening in the market at the time, was that spending some time in the company’s Business Assurance department - both tactically, but also thinking about the longer term, would be a very good route for me to pursue - as I continued to maintain my interests in the Technology & Engineering and Media & Entertainment sectors. So, I decided to do that instead. So after 18 months, I moved across from the company’s Transaction Advisory department to the company’s Business Assurance department.

The Assurance Department was a lot of hard work (I worked mostly in its Investment Management Division), but as I did my C.A., it gave me

  • A great opportunity to learn more about business & finance (something that I didn’t have that much practical or theoreticcal experience of, before, as a Scientist / Engineer)
  • A much better appreciation, given the slowing market and economic context in the U.K., of the different types of challenges and risks that can be faced when running a business.
  • The opportunity to get a well-regarded business qualification, from a well-known global organisation – something I felt would stand me in good stead throughout my career.

Subsequently, the more I worked, the more I found and discovered how practical the finance profession can actually be.

It’s a profession that can sometimes be misunderstood! For example, often people think that those who work in the finance (or investment) professions – all they really care about is making a lot of money. Though there is some truth to this, if you are someone who has worked in finance before, then you have a lot to offer with that experience, with an ability to make the profession, more universally understood and accessible, to members of society - who are perhaps less familiar with the profession, but who are in real need some financial guidance and advice.

So people who have experience of working in finance have a unique and gifted ability to:

  • a) Explain finance in simple terms to a variety of business stakeholders, to help them make better investment and financing decisions.

  • b) More broadly make finance, the finance profession, and its related professions, a force for good, for both business and society.

So looking back on my decision to do an ACA, and not stay in just one department, I’m actually quite glad I took the path that I did. I got a lot from the varied experiences, and still find what I learnt - even though it has been sometime since - very useful to the type of work I do for my customers and clients today.

Question 10

Q10. When you took the path you did at PwC, how was it?

A10. For me, there were both peaks and troughs - something that probably everyone gets with any job!

Before I moved across to the Assurance Department, a lot of people would scare me of the long hours – especially in London. And of how as a junior, I would be given a lot of the mundane work - but that if I wanted to progress, this was just the way things were going to be for a while. I was also told it would be very different to to the kinds of intellectual, and experimental work and experiences I had at Cambridge and MIT.

Reflecting back, I would say that some aspects of this were definitely true! The hours were long, and a lot of the junior level work is dull and mundane. But then, I think, the same is probably true wherever you start - whatever the company or industry. As you get more experience though, the more you learn - and, provided you’re working with talented, co-operative, and supportive people, who are genuinely interested in their own work and your personal development - then more interesting and fulfiling work will come along!

I would say, it’s also true that there can be people who can be difficult to deal with: some quite arrogant, some quite xenophobic: some, you feel, not very competent, and you wonder how they got to where they are in the first place! Whilst these things can easily annoy and frustrate you, it’s important to not be affected by that kind of environment, but to recognise, and instead think how you can best work with these people. Most people in business, have worked very hard to get to where they are, so if these people are still there, they’re probably there for a good reason. So you just have to think about how you can change your own behaviour and workstyle to effectively work with these people.

I guess one thing that I did not like is that, because I had a lot of interests and hobbies: tennis being one of them, film & music, health & fitness, and spending time with friends - being others - because of the long hours, I found these difficult to fit in. Sitting all the exams that pursuing a CA qualification entails also meant having to give up a lot of my weekends. Doing that for 3 years, that was a big sacrifice, and very tough!

If people do end up working in Professional Services, for a company like PwC, (or Deloitte, E&Y, etc.), or any other company like that, early on in their careers, then they should ask themselves what they want from the experience, and how the experience they get fits into their overall career plan or journey! These companies are great training grounds, and provide fantastic opportunities to learn more about areas such as: Transaction Advisory, Business Assurance, or Tax – if working in a Transaction Advisory Department, doing Commercial Due Dilligence work, or becoming a Partner in Business Assurance, or Partner in Tax - in a Big 4 Practice firm environment - is something you really want to do, for the rest of your 40-50 career! For me, personally, after I got my Chartered Qualification, my C.A., I knew that long term that wouldn’t really something that would get me up in the morning!

But if you are truly passionate about one of these areas, and really do feel this is something that will get you up in the mornings, and you would be ok spending all your time, energy, and talents, working in one of these disciplines, for the rest of your life - then I would say, there is absolutely no better place!

I will though say to aspiring graduates, and young professionals, that there is a lot to sacrifice, in terms of extracurricular activities, and possible too, in terms of other exciting career opportunities, in other industries or sectors, (outside the Big 4) that they may be even more passionate about! So, it’s important to keep your eyes wide open to what’s available even outside traditional Professional Services.

It is crucial to try to make the time to get to know people in different fields to your own, just to ensure that your subject, or work, doesn’t become all you think or know about! Over the course of your career, your work should be soul restoring! It should not be soul destroying!

Question 11

Q11. After 6 years, you then left PwC, and chose to do your MBA? Why?

A11.

Having worked in Professional Services, in what ended up being quite a technical role for some years, I just wanted a change in direction. Specifically, I wanted to widen my knowledge of business (beyond what I learnt in the Business Assurance and Transaction Advisory departments at PwC), and also get some experience of what it was like working in other business functions like: Marketing, Human Resources, Advanced Strategic Management, Operations, Technology, Innovation & Entrepreneurship, or other areas in Finance I had not had that much exposure to before (such as: Venture Capital & Private Equity). I also wanted a better exposure to International Commerce - to work abroad for a multi-national company - with their Senior Management and Leadership teams, to help solve an interesting and challenging business problem that had an International focus.

So, these were the main things I wanted to get from my MBA!

I also wanted more of my own time to futher my interests in: the Technology & Engineering, and Media & Entertainment sectors, as those were the sectors I wanted to work in, in the longer term - they’re the sectors I’ve always had a strongest desire to work in:

  • At University, I studied Engineering for 4 years – a subject that is very multi-disciplinary in its outlook - and as much about fostering creative and innovative thinking, through design, as it is about trying to solve technical challenges faced by Engineers & Technologists in different industries. The discipline helps “build bridges”, across “social boundaries”, to those in society who are less fortunate than others.
  • At University, I also fostered a deep interest in Media & Entertainment. I ran my College’s Film Club - the Gonville & Caius Film Society - for 4 years, which was all about hosting and showcasing engaging and entertaining films, for college and university students. I made it a point too to see a few films at the MIT Film Club, when I was in Boston in my 3rd year, watching great conspiracy thrillers like the “Constant Gardener”.

So I left PwC to do my MBA to also help me move closer to the Engineering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment sectors. The shift, along with the experiences from my MBA, would help me move into a challenging business role with an innovative, future thinking company in one of these two sectors. With the experience I had upto that point, it’s a decision I don’t regret!

Question 12

Q12. Now, that you have done your MBA, what do you think it gave you?

A12. I think quite a lot, probably too much to be able to summarise in just one answer. But, if I had to say name two, or three things, I would say:

  • One: A much wider knowledge of business, and greater exposure to Global commerce - as I had originally intended, and hoped for. So, experiences and exposure to areas such as: marketing, human resourcing, strategic management, business consulting, and other areas in finance (such as venture capital & private equity, and mergers & acquisitions), that I did not have much earlier experience in.
  • Second: The realisation that with all I had learnt, that I could have much more impact and enjoyment doing things where my bandwidth was a lot higher! So rather than just working for one or two companies, trying to solve their technical finance or accounting or assurance problems, across the whole year – all the time - I could get to work on a much broader range of problems, in cross-functional teams, in the companies and industries I was most passionate about. It’s something I realised that I would not be able to do if I stayed with a “Big 4 Practice” type firm, like PwC, for the rest of my career.

I guess one extra third thing I got was that my MBA really helped with furthering my own personal development. After the two years, I had become better at working in teams, a better leader, a better networker, and better presenter, as giving talks and presentations to larger audiences - wasn’t something I had to do much of in my earlier roles at PwC.

I think that as I continue to progress in my career, all the things I did learn from my MBA (in terms of: skills developed, knowledge gained, materials acquired, and friends made), these are things that are going to become even more useful and valuable to me, in the years ahead. Having an MEng, CA, and MBA, personally, it’s a very unique and powerful combination - something, I think, very few people can say they have on their CVs…

Lastly, I would say, if anyone is thinking of doing an MBA, and manages to get admitted into well networked, International School, with a strong track record, then provided you work hard, and apply yourself, then it is also “badge of accomplishment” that communicates a very high standard of excellence to others: a strong commercial understanding of business, but also ability to build mutually-respectful business relationships - which are things that going to help you a lot in the future!

Question 13

Q13. What appeals to you specifically about the Engineering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment sectors?

A13. These are the sectors that appeal to me the most, because these are the sectors whose products and services I would like to see more of in the world!

The interest started from a very early age, and was strongly influenced by those around me: my parents, siblings, friends, and wider family - and my interest has developed further since.

Whith Engineering & Technology, I’ve always been very interested in understanding How Things Work! When I was young, for example, I remember my dad buying a book called “The Way Things Work”, by David Macaulay - (published by the very popular education publisher, at the time, Dorling Kindersley (DK)). It was a bookthat really caught my fascination!

Then I remember also enjoying several other Educational CDs my parent’s had bought me, such as the Encarta Multi-Media Encyclopedia, by Microsoft. Its vast repository of digital articles and multi-media videos allowed me to learn a lot more about Science & Engineering. You could store quite a lot in 700MB of memory! Before this, my main source of knowledge of the world, and world history, came from a “12 Volume Britannica Encylopedia Collection” that my grandparents had gifted to me when I was very young.

The Media & Entertainment sector too, has always been a one of interest for me!

When I was young, for example, I remember really enjoying the Violin lessons my mother used to send me to. Then after playing the violin for a sometime, I really enjoyed playing the musical keyboard (a Casio, from Japan) that my Grand Parents got me for my ninth birthday.

And whenever I used to visit them on Holiday, I used to enjoy going for musical keyboard lessons, where I got to learn how to play different kinds of songs. (The song, lambada sticks with me the most!)

Then after learning how to play music, my Dad and Uncle began introducing me to Photography - which is when I first started to learn how to take photos - with a 35mm (Olympus) Camera. A few years after that, I got my first ever digital camera, a Canon PowerShot A30, which I would use to take photos with whenever I went abroad. A few years after this, I then bought my first ever Digital SLR Camera, from Akihabara, (a popular eletronics district) in Tokyo, Japan.

Though I don’t use it as much now, they also bought a Video Camera, which I would sometimes use to take short videos with, whenever we went out for Picnics, or for some Sightseeing, with family friends. Then, the first time I visited Texas, Houston, visiting and seeing my first 3-D film ever at an IMAX, about an adventure in Space, had a lasting impression on me! And so did the other 3-D films I saw later, in other IMAX theatres and cinemas across the world!

Then, of course, after that, I maintained my interest in Films & Music at University, when I helped to run my Colleges Film Society.

So there has always been this very strong creative streak in me, and interest to continue to want to work in the Media & Entertainment sector.

There have been other examples too, of great experiences I’ve had that have been associated with these two sectors. For example:

  • Really enjoying taking up, and getting into a product design class I took when I went to MIT, where, amongst other things, I got to learn more about website design!
  • Then, very vividly remembering how, in another assignments in that class, being tasked with trying to improve the design interface of an American AutomaticTellerMachine (one that would return your card before it tried to dispense any cash, as I’d always get very annoyed by how some ATMs would dispense your card after it dispensed any cash, which meant that if you were in a hurry, you would remember to take your cash, but almost always forget to take your card!)
  • I also remember how in a design & manufacturing class, in one of the projects for that class, being in a design team that was able to design a Yo-Yo with the fewest number of parts - only 3!. It was a really innovative design, and the class professors and supervisors liked it so much, they awarded us class first prize!
  • I have vivid memories too of how I used to stay up till 1 o’clock in the morning, working in the Technology Institute’s main Photography Darkroom Lab, a few floors above the Infinite Corridor, in the main Engineering Building on Campus, trying to develop photo negatives for a class project I was trying to complete, as part of a “Creative Imaging” class I had taken - and one night, having to run through a snow blizzard to try and catch the last shuttle bus from the Zeisiger Sports & Fitness Center, to my Fraternity, Theta Chi, which was all the way across Harvard Bridge on the other side of the Charles River. It was a frigid, and bitterly cold night! But I did get my photos!
  • Then, back in England, in Cambridge, outside of my usual Engineering classes, in my free time, I remember really enjoying running my college’s Film Society. We used to screen a selection of films each week, across of all three terms of the year: Michaelmas, Lent, and Easter, screening films all the way from (i) old classics, such as: Butch Cassidy & the Sundance Kid, and Shawshank Redemption, (ii) contemporary films (contemporary for our generation) such as Pirates of the Caribbean, Lucky Number Slevin, Mystic River, and The Colour Purple, to (iii) foreign films, such as: Amelie, and Swades, to (vi) Anime and Animation type Films, such as: Finding Nemo, and Spirited Away.
  • And I found that really enjoyable! Not only did having a college run film society give University & College students the opportunity to learn new things from watching some really interesting films, (conveniently from inside college), but a chance too, to get a few hours of relieving Entertainment, after a week of intense lectures and practicals.
  • In my years since University, Photography is still something I maintain an interest in, and make a concious effort to maintain, with my Japanese SLR
  • Thinking about the Engineering & Technology projects I have done, the one I did in Fujisawa, near Tokyo, in Japan for a few months, is one that I really enjoyed. It was an opportunity I found through one of my dad’s class mates (they both studied in the same class at IIT, the Indian Institute of Technology). My Dad’s friend was working with Hitachi GST at the time, and felt that there was a project that I could really help with, so invited me to Japan for an 10-week work internship.
  • There, I ended up working with Hitachi GST Senior Management and Leadership, to help build a simulation model that could better quantify the costs and benefits of using a robotic handler across their manufacturing plans, to better automate parts of their Hard Disk Manufacturing production. Apart from the actual work at Hitachi, the trip was also a great opportunity to practice some of my Japanese language skills (which I had taken a class on, at MIT), and learn more about Japan itself: its people, culture, history, food, and natural scenery!
  • Of the more recent Projects, those I did over my MBA, as part of my specialist elective class on “Advanced Strategic Management” class - where, I spent a whole summer learning more about the Electric Car Industry – and how the automotive company, “BMW” could further develop its own Electric cars business - was really interesting. In the same class, I also did an individual dissertation on the Media and Entertainment Company, “Walt Disney” - where I was able to think about and recommend ways I felt the “Walt Disney Company” could better develop and strengthen its product & market portfolio.
  • Then in the Finance electives I took, (just after my “Advanced Strategic Management” elective) I got to research and suggest different ways of improving the performance of the Video Games Company, “Nintendo”. That was really interesting to me, because I got to learn more about Nintendo, and the evolution and changing dynamics of the global “Video Games” industry.
  • Thinking about some more examples in Media, Technology & Entertainment, I enjoyed taking “Drama” for my IGCSEs. In that class, I remember performing really well in a school play we did on “Billy Liar”, which we later then did as a more public performance, at both my school, the British (Embassy) School - and then later, the American Embassy School, (the school that was just across the road from us). I remember being very pleasantly surprised too when I got an A* in Drama - as it wasn’t something was expecting. That moment still leaves me with a very strong desire to want to go and watch musicals and West End Theatre, ever so often!
  • Now, even to the present day, working the way I have, in the years since my MBA, I have focused most of my work search efforts on those companies that I believe to be innovative organisations in the Engineering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment sectors. For example, I worked for 3-months for the UK automotive company, Jaguar Land Rover. After that, I worked for the Marketing & Advertising agency, WPP, in London, across several agencies, for about a year and a half, as they were trying to simplify their business in the face of several challenges being faced by the Marketing & Advertising industry. It was enjoyable work!

So, I think, whilst I could probably do well working across a lot of different sectors (with the skills and experiences I have acquired), the sectors which have appealed to me the most, the ones I have been most passionate about, and really proud of working in, will probably always be the Enginering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment ones. And this is something I want to continue to be able to do!

Question 14

Q14. What keeps you busy now?

A14. Well, soon after my MBA, I decided to go ahead and start my own company. I did this for a few reasons:

After my MBA, the economic outlook in the U.K. became very uncertain. The U.K. public had just voted for “Brexit”, in a national referendum (to leave the European Union), and this resulted in a very sharp slowdown in the U.K, economy. It was really bad luck for me, as companies (in the U.K.), reduced their hiring activities for people at my level (post MBA level)

So, I decided, rather than let my destiny be defined by the whims and fancies of what companies in the U.K. decided, I would focus working more indepedently through my own company - mainly as a vehicle for doing contract work and assignments I found most enjoyable - wherever in the world!

I didn’t want to constrain myself about where, or who I might want to work for, from a Geographic sense. So I set up my own limited company, and started looking for work in areas that I felt I would do well in, and enjoy, such as: “Commercial Finance”, “Marketing”, “Human Resourcing”, “Innovation & Entrepreneurship”, “Operations”, “Manufacturing & Procurement”, and “Business Strategy, and Advanced Strategic Management” - so I could apply what I had learnt from my MBA (which was a big 6k figure, 2-year personal cost / expense for me!)

And as I looked for paid work, I also started to develop some skills in “Artificial Intelligence”, “Machine Learning”, “Data Science”, and “Business Intelligence” - which were becoming more popular and more in demand!

And over this time, working through my own company, I’ve been able to do a lot of amazing work too! For example:

  • Work for a short time for a Charitable District Eye Hospital, to help improve and develop their Internal Management Information systems;
  • Work for an innovative health-drinks start-up, “Happy Ratio”, to help with commercial deal negotiations, as they looked to grow and expand into International markets;
  • Work for an international social and behavioural change communication agency, “Magenta Consulting”, to help with preparing and comparing their (financial) forecasts, and budgets, and acutals, for better investor oversight;
  • Spend some time developing a few tailored (software based) product offerings of my own!

It has all been a lot of hard work, but a lot of really enjoyable work! - working for companies in the Engineering & Technology (Automotive), and Media & Entertainment sectors - applying all of what I have learnt across the years in: Finance, Technology, Data Engineering, Data Science, and Business Intelligence, to some very diverse roles in: the Finance, Marketing, Strategy, and HR departments of these companies. It has been really fulfilling and satisfying work for me!

Outside work:

  • What has been keeping me busy, has just been supporting the local communities in where I live: for example, giving tuition lessons to a young boy in my home town, who needed help with preparing for his UK based, GCSE Maths exams. Then, also helping a nearby couple better prepare their two young toddlers for home schooling lessons - by writing a comprehensive Nursery-level Home-Schooling Guide for them.
  • In terms of my own Health and Fitness outside work, I have no specific goal, but it is something I enjoy doing, so, I try to maintain that, just to try and keep in good tone, and healthy physique.
  • In my spare time, I also enjoy playing sports, racquet sports such as Tennis and Table Tennis, and following International Cricket tournaments, Formula 1 motorsport, watching films (of course!), watching and reading science and technology books and documentaries, and participating in a range of cultural and gastronomic activities with family and friends.

So there are many things outside work that keep me busy! There may be some things I will work on without a clear target, or KPI, just because I enjoy, and am very passionate about those things!

Question 15

Q15. What is going to continue to motivate you?

A15. I think, what is going to continue to motivate me are just my interests and passions, and finding opportunities where I can continue to pursue and achieve these, to a high professional standard.

For me, this means:

  • Getting the opportunity to continue to work with innovative companies, with smart, motivated, and accomplished people, who want to solve tough and challenging problems, that have the potential to change the world for the better. This, of course, is something, I acknowledge, will change over time: with different industry cycles, with different generations of people, it will mean different things to different people. But, personally, this means working hard, so I can keep pushing myself outside of my comfort zone, and keey trying out new things, hopefully still in the Engineering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment sectors
  • I think one other big thing that I would say would also continue to motivate me, is my interest and desire to want to continue to see different parts of the world, and meet new people.
  • I have travelled to quite a few places across Europe, Asia, the Middle East, Southern Africa, and North America. One particularly memorable and enjoyable holiday experience for me was on a wildlife expedition to Botswana - when in one of the campsites, in Linyati Camp, in Chobe National Park, we came in very close contact with pride of lions (the Savuti Pride) - it was such a surreal experience! So, I think, continuing to try and visit new places, and continuing to try to open myself out to new experiences and meet new people, is something that is going to continue to motivate me!

Question 16

Q16. What are your values?

A16. In a sentence, I would say these would be to “work in a way that honors: respect and collaboration, but in way that is still innovative and authentic”.

I don’t see any aspects of this being an “either, or”. Honor, respect, authenticity, collaboration, and innovativeness, are values that have always been very important to me. These are values that, as I have had the time to reflect more and more on who I am as a person, and on what I believe makes me tick, will continue to be values I will try to center my life around.

If someone asked me for a comprehensive list, I would say my values would be to:

  • Be respectful, kind, and loyal to others – to always treat others as I would like to be treated myself
  • Be open-minded to new ideas, and to seek every opportunity to learn new things and embark on new horizons.
  • Always try to keep to every commitment, safely and legally
  • Confront challenges, and not to shy away from taking responsibility for my actions.
  • Believe in being successful, financially, physically, and emotionally - not only for myself, but for but for the benefit of those around me (e.g. friends, family, and wider, and immediate community)
  • Have freedom of expression, and to be creative, but not criticize what I do not understand!
  • Believe that, above all else, to be happy and fulfilled in what I do!

Every day, I try to live up to my values. If I’m honest, I don’t think I will ever succeed at living up to all of them, but I think if I didn’t write them down or read them regularly, I would fail even more miserably in trying to achieve them!

Question 17

Q17. What are the advantages of an MBA?

A17. Having gone through one myself, I think there are many. It’s tough to summarise all of them in one go, but, here are a few:

  • One, I think, are the important lessons you learn, from studying the successes or failures of variety of different businesses in the classroom from the Professors. Though, you may not, in these hours, be working on any actual live business project, this classroom learning is still very important, as what you learn are the findings of research from studying 100s of different companies, rather than what you might learn from just one long case study, or one isolated business project.
  • Second, I would say are the people. The MBA people I met and worked with at my school were from all over the world (from over 30 different nationalities, in my year group alone!). I think, when you have that opportunity to work in a melting pot of such a diverse group of people, you learn a lot from their different perspectives, and also, for example, of how important something like “culture” is in business planning and decision making, especially in International contexts. In fact, with the people you meet, what you might achieve, by going on such a programme, are friends who may potentially go on to become your friends for the rest of your life!
  • Third, I would say are the opportunities you get for extra-curricular development. You’ll learn a lot in the classroom, but your learning shouldn’t just be confined to books, libraries, and business case studies. When I did my MBA, I personally felt I got quite a lot through its extra-curricular activities. They helped develop in me, a much stronger attitude and confidence of wanting to push myself, and try out new things!

For example, I got the opportunity to:

  • Mentor and teach students on the basic principles of finance and accounting to help my classmates get through our Accounting, Corporate Finance, and Economics classes, by becoming a member of the school’s Banking & Finance society
  • Coach my classmates through business consulting case studies, by becoming a member of the school’s Consulting Club
  • Represent my school in a Private Equity Case Competition that was being held in Amsterdam, Holland, as part of the specialist elective I took on “Venture Capital & Private Equity”. My team didn’t win the competition, but we did learn some valuable lessons!
  • Take part in (and at times co-captain) our Table Tennis team as we prepared for the annual Sports Tournament, MBAT, in France, a tournament that was participated in by MBA schools across Europe
  • Help with social events, whenever I could fit in the time.

I was able to get involved in all these things, because I had already learnt and knew about many of these things from my earlier work experiences - and so I was in a much stronger position to impart this knowledge, and teach and coach my classmates as a member of the school’s Banking & Finance Society, and Consulting Club.

Personally, when it comes to thinking about the advantages of such a course, I think there are so many strands of personal development, leadership, and communication that you will get, just by being in an environment with a lot of smart and motivated people - every day - for two years - that it is that hive of activity that you will get from an MBA!

Question 18

Q18. What is the typical student that goes on an MBA?

A18. I think, it varies quite a lot. I don’t think there is a typical student, but they say the average age is 28. I think, from my experiences, that it might range from 23 to 35! (I did my MBA when I just turned 30).

Some students, quite a large percentage of them, might have worked previously in invement Banking or some kind of Business Consulting. (I had come from a Professional Services / Technology & Engineering background). But its not always the case. Some might have been ex-army personnel. Some of them might have worked for social enterprises, or have been entrepreneurs, now wanting to try something new and different. Some might have been working in family businesses. There may also be some who were doctors, who now want to learn how to run or manage their own clinic. So, it really is quite diverse!

I think when it comes to people wanting to further educate themselves, sometimes people might think they’re already very successful in their profession, and that they are already learning a lot, so will just stay where they are. This might be true! But, I think, by going onto an MBA programme, at least from what I experienced, you just learn from more people, with a much greater diversity and perspective. And that’s a valuable experience you can take with your for the rest of your working career.

Question 19

Q19. Do you see that it is much more competitive to get onto an MBA course?

A19. I think it is competitive, but I don’t think the competitiveness is necessarily in terms of having the best resume by traditional standards. I don’t think MBA recruiters are always looking for those people with the highest grades at school, or for those who might have worked at a certain kind of company, or within any specific kind of industry before. They’ll have some criteria around this, but it won’t just be around this!

I do however think that it is competitive, in terms of their search for people with great personality and character, and what these kind of people can bring to their time at Business School. I think many schools would rather have somebody from a non-traditional background, who has the openness to learn, and willingness to mentor and teach other people in their MBA class cohort.

I also think that sometimes people mistakedly think of competition as being something that excludes people from lowly backgrounds. I don’t think anybody is ever excluded from an MBA programme. I think those people who might be excluded, are those who are not willing to contribute to the community, rather than those people who don’t have or come from a particular kind of social, educational, or ethnic background.

Question 20

Q20. What are the most successful character properties to be a business owner and leader?

A20.

  1. I think, number one would be humility and responsiveness to feedback!

    I think, often, as a leader, you can sometimes think that because you’re the leader, you’ve got to the top, you think that you know more than these other people. But, I think, a lot of what you get from an organization is actually from tapping into the other people! (otherwise, you’re just interacting with yourself!)

    Related to the above, I also think that a leader who is too autocratic is one who is using the power of his employees. When I was on a tour, for example, at one of the Hitachi GST factories in Japan, one of the Engineers there showed me how a certain part of the production process for making hard disk drives came from the company’s employee’s suggestion scheme. It wasn’t the leader, or Chief Technology Officer, or Chief Executive Officer, who thought, I know best, let me design it myself. Instead, the leader listened to his or her employees.

  2. I think number two, would be, to be creative.

    I recently read, over one of these summers, a book called Zero to One, by Peter Thiele, the founder of PayPal, written for those wishing to learn more about what it takes to found a break-through company. Peter Thiele says in the book that the most difficult thing is moving from Zero to One, rather than from One to Hundred. Peter Thiele created the first online mobile payment system - a Zero to One move. Now, there can be lots of replica business ideas, moving from One to a Hundred, but, to do the first one, is extremely challenging! So, Peter Thiele understood the importance of being creative, and being able to think outside the box.

  3. Third, I think, just as with many other things, is commitment and focus!

    You can’t be all things to all people! Peter Thiele thought let us focus on launching PayPal, rather than thinking, I’ve got 100 ideas and let me try to look at them all.

Question 21

Q21. What are your strengths and what are your weaknesses?

A21.

I think, one strength I have is commitment. I think, in whatever it is that you want to do, you need be focused and committed. In athletics classes, for example, you might find people who run to a line, but then stop half the way, and then start walking. In business (and life, more generally), I don’t think that approach is going to get you much success! Whatever it is that you want to do, you should be wholly committed, and focus, and pursue that thing with a real sense of confidence, clarity, and conviction!

Number Two, I think, is the ability to be open-minded. Presenting information very formally in front of large audiences, and writing lots management reports wasn’t something that came naturally to me, before I did my MBA (it was not something I had to do much of in my roles at PwC). But later, as I got more practice and experience, it was something I really came to like. So just being open-minded about trying new things, I think, is very important.

Number three, I think, is just being positive. Having a positive mindset, I think, is critical for success on a lot of dimensions. You can start something new and then think, this is difficult, I’m going to give up! Or you can think, this is something that actually is difficult, but I like the challenge, I am going to persist with it, try, and try again, until I succeed!

Question 22

Q22. How do you put yourself into that positive frame of mind? What do you do?

A22. Part of the way I do this is by practicing gratitude. I think that other people often think, in life, that “happy people” are just born lucky - for example, that they were just born into a wealthy family. But happiness is a choice!

I think there are many things that we can be grateful for. For example, I think that a lot of people could, at any point, pay for somebody to cook for them, and then have that person clean up for them afterwards …they can go to a restaurant. But, I think that this is something that many people still take for granted, because they are so used to it! For example, I can turn on the tap, and get clean water. So, that is something I too am very grateful for!

I think that the act of practicing gratitude is something that is still very counter-cultural in many countries and societies - because we always like to complain about poor customer service. But the act of practicing gratitude requires great discipline!

Question 23

Q23. Is being where you are now a childhood dream?

A23. Absolutely not!

I think, when I was young, I used to watch a lot of Steven Spielberg films, and read Science Fiction Novels - those by Arthur C. Clarke, Jules Verne, Isaak Asimov, and H.G. Wells. Then, after seeing Jurassic Park in the early 1990s, a lot of Cartoon Network, Hannah Barbara productions, lots of Disney Films, and some Futuristic Sci-Fi Hollywood for the first time, that I wanted to become a Film Director, or Rocket Scientist. Then after watching a lot of Ally McBeal, and TV series like Due South, during my adolescent years, that perhaps I wanted to become a lawyer so I could go to trial and do those debates, or be a Mounty Police Officer with a Siberian Husky, called “Diefenbacker”, just like that person on Due South.

A few years later, when I then went to University to study Engineering, after 4 years, I then went to work in Professional Services, to get a different kind of perspective and experience.

And having worked in Professional Services, after a few years, I was looking for just a new kind of experience, something that was more commercial, and less technical, that would give me a better exposure to international commerce.. So, I then did my MBA, to meet new people, work on problems in new industries, to learn more widely about business, and to try to find out more about the topics, companies, and industries that interested me the most.

And since then, I have been working through my own company, and engaging in, and with the topics, companies, and industries I am most passionate about!

Question 24

Q24. What does the future hold for you?

A24. Thats a great question!

The honest answer is that I don’t know, and I think that is what makes life so exciting, - you never know what is going to be next.

A decade ago, I don’t think I would ever have known that I would be where I am now. When I was young, I think I wanted to be a film director or rocket scientist!

At University I did Engineering, and after 4 years said to myself that I wasn’t going to do further academic study for sometime, I’m going to go into Professional Services.

But now, I’m steering myself towards the Engineering & Technology, and Media & Entertainment sectors.

Before I never used to be that much into sport and health and fitness, but I am now more into it.

So, again, I think it is impossible to predict the future.

I think, what makes life very exciting is that you want to remain open-minded. Therefore, if new opportunities come along, I don’t think one should say say: “oh I never thought of this, let me just ignore this”. I think, we want to still be opened to it, but we still want to maintain our focus! So, it doesn’t mean we are going to be “blown by the wind”!, but if a new opportunity did come along, and one decided that that opportunity was for them, then I think one should commit to that fully!

Question 25

Q25. What made the person you are today?

A25. I think I am just the product of all of their past experiences. Was it Chuck Palanuick, the American Novelist, who said, “Nothing of me is original. I am the combined effort of everybody I’ve ever known”

I personally think I’ve just been extremely lucky, to have worked, over the years, with some excellent friends, coaches, and mentors, who have instilled in me some great character!

I think this is just huge luck of the people I’ve experienced, which is why I think that meeting new people, and opening myself out to different experiences is critically important for my own personal development!

Question 26

Q26. What is your definition of wealth and a successful life?

A26. I think there is no clear definition because it means different things to different people!

For some people, financial wealth is very important, not necessarily because they are materialistic - but because it might be that their family never had wealth, and this is one way they can achieve that level of comfort.

(Financial security for me, because it’s something I’ve never had, or struggled with the past few years, because of Brexit, in the United Kingdom has become important for me!)

For other people, it might be with their career.

For others, it might be with their health and fitness, and so forth.

I think if there was a definition - it would be an intangible and general one - just to be happy and fulfilled, in what you do - to achieve everything and anything that you want and need, to truly put your mind to! That will mean different things to different people!

I think, if you wake up and you feel, “I’m grateful and excited to have this day”, then that is a sign of success!

However, I think if you wake up and you think, “I just need to get through this day”, then that is a sign you might want to do something different.
