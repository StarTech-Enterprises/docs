Experiences to date - UofCambridge, PwC, Manchester Business School, Jaguar Land Rover, WPP, and Diageo!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 09/12/2019

A big thanks to Harsh Batra (Vice President of Ethos Data) for arranging this! And also to all the other great people - colleagues, coaches, mentors, and friends - I’ve had the opportunity to work with so far in my career!

Sachin Tandon is one of the smartest, humblest, guys I have ever known. In this wide ranging conversation, we go back in time, to unravel his career journey to date - how, and why, he ended up studying and working at the places he did (at some very well-known institutions across the world), and then after some years working as a company employee, took the big leap into independent freelancing, to see if that is where he could work for companies and industries on the problems he was most passionate about.

I personally see him as a collector of experiences. Someone with a strong foundation of skills - that takes decades to acquire - but underlying it all, a childish curiosity to want to keep learning and keep growing - with humility, focus, and gratitude at its very core.

The skill sets, and dimension of understanding he brings to businesses, (in his freelancing capacity, “Star Tech Enterprises”) is something very unique to the business universe! If you get the opportunity to work with him, I’d say you, and your business, are very lucky!

You can read all about him, in this interview below. It’s quite an length, but very comprehensive read, and definitely all worth it!

Introduction

Harsh Batra - (VP of Ethos Data)

In this one to one, Sachin Tandon talks through some of his career highlights to date: from his early experiences in Business Consulting, Assurance, and Finance at PwC, in London, to his then later experiences in International Commerce at Manchester Business School, in Manchester, U.K., to then some of his more recent entrepreneurial stints, working with companies such as Jaguar Land Rover, WPP, Diageo, the Omnicom Media Group.

Sachin currently freelances through his own small company, Star-Tech Enterprises, a small venture he set up, with the mission of wanting to “to support businesses around the world – large multi-nationals, medium-sized companies, and small and innovative start-ups – to achieve more!” – He particularly focuses on those companies in the Technology & Engineering, and Media & Entertainment sectors.

Sachin has led quite a “non-linear” career to date. After completing a degree in General Engineering at the University of Cambridge, U.K., and then M.I.T., Boston, the U.S., he began his graduate career at the Professional Services firm, PriceWaterhouseCoopers (PwC), in London, the U.K. After this, he did his MBA from Manchester Business School, the U.K., before then taking the very “entrepreneurial leap”, of turning his home into his own start-up / freelancing headquarters, to pursue what he felt like, was a small gap in the market, and also strongly growing area of interest for him.

What follows is a transcript of my conversation with Sachin, where along with some his career highlights, he shares some important life lessons, and tells us how it’s all going, and what he hopes to do next.

Sachin thanks for taking the time to do this!

Hello Harsh! Thanks for having me!

So, Sachin, just looking at your career history to date, you’ve definitely gone on and done a lot of different things - more than I might possibly say of most – but it’s clear to me at least, that you have taken a strong liking to business. As a younger boy, when you were growing up, did you always have an ambition to get into business? Was it something always on the radar when you were coming through the schooling system you grew up in?

No, not really. When I was young, I was always quite focused on my schoolwork. Outside school, in my free time, I used to watch a lot of films by Stephen Spielberg, and read books by H.G. Wells, Jules Verne, Isaak Asimov, and Arthur C. Clarke. I thought one day, I wanted to learn more about what Film Directors did, or some create whacky flying gadget, or flying machine, and become some kind of Engineering Rocket Scientist when I was younger. But then also, after watching TV series like Ally McBeal, and Due South, I thought I might want to become some kind of Lawyer, or be a Mounty Police Officer, with a Siberian Husky, called Diefenbaker.

It was only until much later, perhaps 22 or 23, when I started to better understand what a few of these professions really meant – that I felt I might want to spend most of my career, not just researching and experimenting, like what many Engineering Rocket Scientists might do, but a career where I could also spend more time working with people, doing innovative and creative things, - sometimes crazy things – but in a career where I could help other people – which is really, what I think, a successful career in business is all about!

That’s great. You’ve achieved quite a lot in your career so far – and I see gone on to some pretty impressive places, and done work equally impressive. What, in your own mind, do you think you would put your success down to?

Well, if I had to name just one thing, it would be tenacity!

Throughout my career, I’ve recognised the importance of dealing with both success and failure. And where I’ve met with success, I’ve tried to build on those successes, but at the same time, just as importantly, whenever I’ve failed, I’ve always tried to ensure I’ve learnt from the experience – so I can “bounce back” from those not so good experiences, learn from them, and have another go!

That tenacity (or perseverance, if you will), along with the ability to communicate clearly, with empathy, and form strong relationships with people, I would say have been the critical success factor to any life and work success I think I’ve had so far.

I remember once, in a work interview, I was once asked: what is the one trait I would most like to be remembered for. And, at the time I can remember saying: “Just never giving up”. I think that one trait, has and always be something, I will carry with me.

Thanks Sachin! Do you think it is these qualities that have allowed you to get on well with the people you have worked with so far?

I think it was one of the really important things, for definite.

When I think about the people I really admire and look up to, we often both have a number of things in common, but also a number of things that are different too. But that idea of “Just never giving up”, and always “Striving for excellence” are qualities we both have often shared.

University - Engineering

I see that after your primary and secondary schooling, you went to University, and did a Master’s Degree in Manufacturing Engineering. After school, why did you decide to do that and pursue Higher Education? How was it?

For me, I just saw going to University as a natural next step from Further Education and schooling system I had grown up in. I had worked hard (put in a lot of time, energy, and effort) into my “A-level” subjects, and then early on in my second year of Sixth Form College, knew that I was also on track to getting high grades in the subjects I took.

At the time, my thinking was that I wanted to follow in my Father’s and Grandfather’s footsteps and pursue my Higher Education in Engineering. Both my Dad and Grandfather had studied Engineering when they went to college. Both had worked as Engineers early on in their careers.

My Grandfather was a Civil Engineer and used to design and build Hydro-Electric dams, in India in the 1970s and 1980s, because he aspired to reduce the country’s reliance on Fossil Duels.

My Father started his career working as a Chemical Engineer for companies like Dunlop, and Slazenger, designing innovative Tennis balls and Tennis racquets – Racquets that were made from lighter composite materials, and Tennis Balls filled with more complex gases that made the them last longer and bounce better. After Dunlop, he then worked for some years at companies such as: Bowater, Rexam, and Bonnar, to help them design and make polythene rolls – those used for wrapping the Bales of Hay you see dotted around the English countryside, before then moving onto a more International career in General Management.

So, aspiring to follow a similar path, I basically also thought I too would want to go to a well-known University, to read Engineering. A University that was well-resourced, had a good facilities, and admitted a lot of motivated, smart, and ambitious students, who I could learn a lot from.

After doing some research, I saw that Cambridge was highly ranked in the League Tables, and that both its Engineering course, and Undergraduate Teaching had a very strong global reputation. I was also left with a positive impression after visiting them at an Open-Day. So, I decided to try my luck and apply. Then, after a couple of interviews, I was lucky enough to be accepted on their 4 Year Masters of Engineering programme, at the college, Gonville & Caius.

As to how it was, overall, I had a really enjoyable time. My college, Gonville & Caius was one of the smaller ones – but still very idyllic, and cosy. It was conveniently located close to the City Centre, where all the shops were, and wasn’t very far either from the University’s main Engineering department.

The college itself had a very nice, close-knit, community feel to it! Our supervisors and professors were really wonderful people - very passionate about teaching, about the subjects they taught, and about the well-being of its students. Gonville & Caius college itself had a very diverse mix of International students, with students that read not just Engineering, but subjects ranging all the way from: Medicine, Mathematics, Natural Sciences, Languages, Music, Economics, Philosophy, Computer Science, to the Classics, and English Literature.

It also had, what some people today might call, mini celebrities – for example, I distinctly remember meeting Stephen Hawking, the Theoretic Physicist and Cosmologist, a few times; he was an Honorary fellow of the college. Then, I also think Ken Clark, the Conservative Party politician, and for a brief time, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, was also a student of Gonville & Caius, where he had read for his degree in Law.

Going back to what I did, I would say the Engineering course at the University was very tough – very theoretical, and underpinned by a lot of very advanced maths - where we were challenged to solve a lot of very complex Maths & Engineering problems (Example Papers, or P-Sets, as they used to call them).

And, then, because I did well in my first and second years, in my third year, I was selected to go onto the competitive exchange program at the Technology Institute, M.I.T. in Boston, the U.S.A. – which was another great experience. After that, I then returned to the U.K. to complete the fourth and final year of my master’s degree.

Overall, I would say, apart from the lectures, and classroom teachings, I found the various lab practicals, and industrial visits to companies, to be very enjoyable and interesting. And, academically, overall, I graduated with a 1st class – which is something I was very pleased with.

So, yes, overall, an all-round great experience!

Professional Services - PwC

Well, Sachin, that sounds really amazing! It certainly sounds like you had an enjoyable time - lots of hard work, lots of study, but also a lot of opportunity to work with some very diverse, smart and driven people. After you graduated, I see your first full time work experiences were with PwC. How did you end up taking your first steps in the working world, with them?

As with anything, mostly from the work opportunities I had been looking for and which had come to me at the time. As I was thinking about what and where I might want to work next, as a new graduate, I became quite driven by a desire, at least at the start of my career, to want to work for a well-known, well-established global company, that would provide a strong platform and foundation from which I could build my future career.

I had enjoyed some of my earlier Research & Development experiences, in my gap year, before University, and then also over the summers, during University, when I had worked for companies such as: Renishaw, a High Tech Engineering Company, near Bristol, in the U.K., and then after that, the National Broadcasting & Media Company, the BBC, in their Central Communications, and Technology department, in London, the U.K..

I had enjoyed too some of the “Engineering Consultancy” type projects I did, as part of my degree course, in my third and fourth years at University, where I got to visit, just-in-time factories at companies such as: Coty Lipsticks, IMI Norgren, and Rolls Royce, in the U.K., and then later, Hitachi GST, in Japan - to try and help improve certain aspects of their manufacturing activities – e.g. in reducing changeover times on assembly lines, achieving better line balancing between lines, reducing WIP & Inventory levels, reducing lead times, or trying to increase product throughput rates, and so on.

So, when I started thinking ahead, towards the end of my degree, about what I might want next, that was when many of those close to me (family, close friends and classmates, etc.) had encouraged me to consider applying for a full-time job in the field of what was called “Management Consultancy” – to see if that was something I might like do next. There would be some overlap with the skills I had developed, and Engineering experiences I had gained, but also, given my, till then, very Research & Development like experiences, provide me with a newer, and slightly different perspective of business. So, at the time, because I was interested in broadening and deepening my experiences, I thought, why not?

So, that’s when I started focusing my application efforts to that field, and then after applying to a few places, received an offer to work, in my penultimate summer (the summer before the start of my final University year), at a company called PwC, in a their “Transaction Advisory” department, and in that department, for a small division called “The Strategy Group”.

I had an enjoyable time that summer – it was quite different to my earlier gap year, and internship experiences, but still interesting – doing desk-based “Commercial Due Diligence” work for different kinds of companies.

The particular division I worked in, “The Strategy Group”, would often get its work from outside Investment Companies. They would often approach us, because they needed an independent and external team, such as ours, to carry out some independent market research for them, about a particular target company and its market (e.g., about the products and services the company sold, what its positioning was vis a vis its closest competitors, what the latest market trends and developments affecting the company might be, what customer’s opinions about the company were, and any regulatory developments on the horizon, that might affect the industry’s and company’s future growth).

At the end of that summer, the division I had been working in, had extended me an offer to work for them, on a full-time basis. Because I didn’t want to start the fourth year at University without a job offer in hand, I accepted their offer to work full time. Amongst other things, I felt that having an offer in hand would allow me focus much more on the course materials of my 4th year. It allwed me to spend more time planning and preparing for my Manufacturing Engineering Project trip to Japan - where I had gone to work for Hitachi GST for 10 weeks, near Tokyo, on a challenging and interesting just-in-time factory automation project. So, I was quite content with all the decisions I had made.

Again, really great to hear Sachin. Just after you graduated, how did you feel before you moved down from Cambridge to London? I imagine you must have been both quite excited, but also a little nervous, about starting your first full time job?

Well, yes, that’s very true. I felt both excited, but also a little nervous and anxious.

Excited because I knew PwC were considered as one of the top graduate employers in the country, quite well known in many areas in business. I used to often see in the news, news reporters quoting statements from Market Research reports that had been written and published by PwC.

I also knew PwC were a global company, with a large network of international offices, so it would mean opportunities too of working on projects, with people from very different backgrounds and countries, all very motivated to work and learn, which I also saw as a plus!

The offer too was in London, which I was excited about. The city, as a capital city, is considered to be a thriving epicentre for business. There being more commerce and companies, there are more opportunities for career development and progression. Away from business, the city itself is also very diverse and cosmopolitan, with no shortage of things for someone in their mid 20s to do!

But, yes, you’re absolutely right, I was a bit nervous too. But I think this is only natural, and happens whenever you start a new job, move to different city or place, meet new people, etc., for the first time. There are so many new things to find out. There are lots of things you have to learn, very, very quickly. It takes time to settle in.

So, you started at PwC, working in a division called “The Strategy Group”, in the companies “Transaction Advisory” department, doing mostly “Commercial Due Dilligence” work, for external Investment Companies (PE firms, VC firms, sometimes listed Corporates). Then later, I see you started working towards obtaining a Chartered Qualification, in Finance & Accountancy. How did that come about?

Well, yes, that’s exactly right. Just as I had done in the summer before, I stayed with company’s “The Strategy Group” division doing “Commercial Due Diligence” work (for Investment Companies), for about another year.

However, as I met with people from other departments (those closest to me were the “Financial Due Diligence”, and “Market Valuation & Strategy” divisions, within the “Transaction Advisory” department), and learnt more about what these people did, that’s when I discovered how quite a lot of them also held a “Chartered Qualification” – and that many of those at my level in other divisions were also going through separate, structured, training programs to try to obtain one.

From a long-term career standpoint, I felt that getting a Chartered Qualification would definitely stand me in good stead. The CA Qualification, what PwC encourages a lot of its graduates to do, is a well-respected business qualification; is internationally very well recognised; and, also, as you go through the training of the qualification, teaches you a lot of the key skills needed to run a successful business. So, for me, I guess, you could say, I saw the CA in particular as a great opportunity to learn more about things I never really knew much about before: Finance & Accoutancy, which appealed to the entrepreneurial streak in me!

I also felt, after doing the kind of “Commercial Due Dilligence” assignments for about 18 months, that my natural interests, and strengths were starting to gravitate towards solving the more complex kind of “Finance” problems that people in the other departments had been working on, even though I was becoming quite skilled in doing “Commercial Due Dilligence” work!

And, the more I learnt about the Qualification and what people did, the more intrigued I became by what I didn’t know. If I’m honest, it reminded me of some of the kinds of problems I had to solve at some of the just-in-time factories I worked on, when I did my Engineering degree at University.

So, after doing some research, and deciding to make the move, I asked some of the Partners in my division whether it would be possible for me to pursue a CA. Some of the Partners really understood the kind of work I really liked and enjoyed, or how easily bored I would get without a tough challenge, and where my natural strengths and interests were. Many of them encouraged me to find out more. Some did say that a Chartered Qualification might not be something I could pursue from within their division, because of some of the conditions of the Qualification – that you had to do work that was more closely linked to what “Finance” people did, rather than what “Commercial Due Dilligence” people did – which kind of made sense!

So, they introduced me to some of the Partners in the “Financial Due Dilligence” division. Unfortunately, the Partners there said I had just missed out on the deadline for that year’s intake, and that if I wanted to start the qualification with them, would have to wait for another year. So then, after that, I got some introductions to Partners in some of the other divisions. That’s when, I think, I met quite an open minded Partner from Australia, who said they still had some room for a few people to work in the company’s “Investment Management Group” division, and that they would be willing to take me on, and sponsor me for my CA!

So, that’s when I quite enthusiastically took up the offer, and then ended up moving across from the company’s “The Strategy Group” division to the company’s “Investment Management Group” division

Though the work was a little different in the “Investment Management Group” division (more mathematical / financial in nature, and had involved a lot more travel to client offices – and so I was developing a different skill set) – the work was still quite interesting. I was able to learn quite a lot, particularly in topics such “Finance & Accountancy”, “Law”, and “Tax” - business topics that were all completely new to me! I was also getting some practical experience in learning how to value different kinds of “Financial Investments” (there are many different ways!), and how to build different kinds of “Financial Acquisition” models (there are many different models! - VC models, Private Equity models, LBO models, etc..). The experience too, of working for different clients, also gave me a better appreciation, first hand, of some of the challenges and risks that might be faced when running a business.

I also came to the realisation that Finance is something that sits at the beating heart of any business, and is area that is very important to understand, and get to grips with, in business, more generally!

So, after working in the firm’s “Strategy Group Division”, I ended up working in the firms “The Investment Management Group” division for 3 years, to do and complete my CA training!

I’m so happy to hear that Sachin! I know from a lot of people that the CA can be a very challenging qualification to get. Any Chartered Qualification, in any domain, especially one that takes 3 years to obtain, is a challenge to get! It’s something that needs a lot of dedication, focus and commitment, not just because of all the tough exams you have to sit – but also because you have to work in a demanding full-time job at the same time! So, congratulations on persisting with that! So, I wonder, after you qualified, and got your Chartered Qualification, what did you decide to do next?

Well, the day I qualified and got my CA, I went out and celebrated with friends who went through the same training. I remember going out and buying myself a very expensive bottle of Champagne - (a Dom Perignon, I distinctly remember)! Thar was a great feeling!

In terms of actual work, I wasn’t entirely sure what I wanted to do next. Quite a few people in my peer group, in my division, moved across to other departments. Some left the company to pursue their own thing. My bosses in “The Investment Management Group” were quite pleased with the progress I had been making, and work I was doing. I think they also saw me as a very valuable team member that was giving them good continuity to my then main client – “The Wellcome Trust” – the London based, Medical Research Charity.

Personally, I also didn’t feel like I needed to move anywhere else immediately. I had established a good relationship with the various people in the Finance and Investment departments at “The Wellcome Trust”. I also felt like I was doing good work, still getting some good work experience, still learning new things, and able also to develop my management, supervisory, coaching and mentoring skills, as other, less experienced members joined my team. So, I decided to stay, with my division for another 2 years – and ended up working with the “Investment Management Group”, in London, for a full 5 years.

It was a pretty amazing extra 2 years too! I continued to work for my main client, the Medical Research Charity “The Wellcome Trust”, doing finance type of work (financial modelling, financial valuations, financial negotiations, financial assurance, financial advisory work, etc.), and was able to work for a variety of other companies too! For example, for the Engineering Construction Company (Balfour Beauty) – on a Renewable Energy Fund they were looking to set up; for the Pharmaceutical Company (Johnson & Johnson); for two Commercial Real Estate Companies (Land Securities, and Deutsche Bank RREEF); For a short time, the firms “Sustainability & Climate Change Group” division – working on a project for the IMF (International Monetary Fund), to help them create a new Climate Investment Fund to invest in new Renewable Energy projects – which was something I was interested in being a part of… thinking about the work my Grandfather did in designing and building Hydroelectric Dams.

Well Sachin, it certainly sounds like you got involved in plenty of projects and areas, working for companies across quite a few sectors. When you were there, working towards both your Qualification AND for the company’s Clients, how was juggling that? What would you now say to other people, perhaps new graduates or other young professionals, who might be aspiring to follow a very similar path, early on in their career?

Well, I would say, as with any path, there can be both peaks and troughs, and I found that to be the case with my experiences.

Before I started at PwC, some people would warn me of the sometimes very long hours I would have to work, both in the company’s “Transaction Advisory” department, but particularly the company’s “The Investment Management Group” division – and that too, the CA, at a Professional Services firm, was no easy thing to get! They warned me of how hard and challenging it would be!

I will admit, the hours were long. That I can say! But I think, the hours were probably just as long in the company’s other divisions too. I also think that when you’re starting something new, whatever company or industry that may be in, and when “high achievement” is your goal, when do you not have to put in some long hours?

Initially, when I started out, I did sometimes find working as the most “junior” person quite frustrating (as is probably only natural when you are the youngest person in the office!). You can end up being given a lot of mundane work, and you might not get to participate in as many client meetings, as you might like to, compared to those more senior to you. But, again, I think, the same is probably true at a lot of places, and depends heavily on what knowledge and skills you already have, and what you can realistically, from your experiences, bring to the table!

Overall, when I look back at those years, I did feel like I was able to learn a tremendous amount, from both the formal training I went through, but also some of the people I worked with – motivated, smart, very hard working – some of whom I am still in quite close touch with today!

In these kinds of companies, you can end up working with people – managers, partners, and so forth - who can be really difficult to deal with. But I think, it is important to not let that affect your work, or for you to be affected by that kind of environment!

One thing I would say that I did not like, was that I had a lot of outside interests and hobbies: Tennis, Cricket, Formula 1, Films & Music, Health & Fitness, and Cooking, amongst others. I also wanted to spend more time socialising with friends, outside of work, and to visit more places, socially, in London.

But because of the long hours, I personally found these difficult to fit in. The volumes of work can be very high, and when working in the firm’s Assurance department, had to work to fairly pressing deadlines. And trying to maintain this, whilst working towards obtaining my CA, for 3 years, I did not find easy. It wasn’t impossible, but it was a lot of hours and a lot hard work, and I had to sacrifice quite a lot in terms of my social life and social activities, (work across a lot of weekends, for a full 3 years) – which was tough!

So, what I would say to others that might be considering following a similar path?

I think it really depends on what you want from the experience, and how you think it will help with your long-term career goals. Your career goals are likely to change and shift overtime – this is only natural! Everyone’s career goals change slightly, in some way, over time!

But, I think, you should try to think about how, from the experiences you will get, however many years that might be, what pathways and roles those experiences will open up for you afterwards - should you do decide to leave, and go on to do something else.

So, try to get some advice beforehand - from friends, career coaches, experienced mentors, potential sponsors, or other people you trust. Doing this, and seeing how the experience fits into your Master Plan, and Bigger Career Picture, will definitely help you become more committed and convinced about any decisions you have to make. This will in turn help you to move forward with a stronger sense of purpose and conviction!

When I look back at my own experiences: - after getting a lot of encouragement from family and friends, to apply to companies and roles they felt would give me a new insight into Business, different from the mainly “Research & Development” experiences I had till then, I decided to apply to the Professional Services companies, and got an offer to start working for PwC to get that different perspective.

The offer I got gave me me a great chance to work for a well-known, well-regarded company, one that was large enough that it gave me the opportunity to work with people from different countries and backgrounds, as we worked together to solve tough and challenging business problems.

And in the 5 to 6 years I was there, I felt I had achieved quite a lot:

  • I got an interesting mixture of experiences in Finance, and Business Consulting, from the company’s “Business Assurance” and “Transaction Advisory” departments.
  • I came out with a Chartered Qualification, a CA – a well-recognised qualification, that I had worked really hard to get, and which taught me a lot about Finance & Accountancy, and about Tax.
  • I felt like I had worked for quite a wide range of companies too, (both financial, and non-financial – private and public, and some listed FTSE 100 companies) - companies I felt I may not have gotten the opportunity to work for had I perhaps worked elsewhere
  • It gave me too a much better appreciation of the different kinds of risks that might be faced when running a business

I think it has definitely helped me towards achieving my long term career goals – of “one day” being able to work as a Senior Business Leader, for a company in the “Engineering & Technology”, or “Media & Entertainment” sectors.

But going back to answering the original question - what I would say to others considering pursuing a similar path: well, the hours can be long and intense. And in the early years (not months, but 2 to 3 years), you will likely have to sacrifice quite a lot in terms of the other extra-curricular activities you might want to pursue.

That said, the hard work you do put in will eventually pay off. You will definitely learn about Business, and you will definitely, if you decide to do your CA Qualification, get to work on solving some pretty “complex” problems, and learn more about “Finance & Accountancy”. So, it is something that will definitely provide you with a very strong foundation to build on for the rest of your career.

But, I would also say that this is just the path that I happened to choose, from the opportunities that came to me at different moments. Other people, in their own minds, might want to pursue slightly different paths. Some people may already have in their minds a very strong “sector focus” and know from the outset what “sector” they want to work in, and focus all their application efforts there. Then, once they are in that sector, I guess, what happens after that, will just depend on what they want to do and what “roles” they think might want to work in, from the opportunities they create for themselves afterwards.

Business School - MBA

Thank you for sharing that Sachin! I’m sure some of our readers will really appreciate you taking the time to share your experiences in this way – I personally, don’t think very many people, from the outset, ever really figure out their entire career path out completely, or ever try to be too savvy about following a career path in exactly the way they might have originally imagined. But everyone eventually does find their own path, and true compass.

So, tell me more. As you were working at PwC, why after 6 years did you decide to leave your role, to pursue your MBA?

Really, the honest answer is that I just saw it as another challenge. Getting a Chartered Qualification was a challenge, I found my work, initially, in PwC’s “Transaction Advisory” department quite a challenge. Then the work in their “Business Assurance” department, in the “Investment Management Group” division also a challenge. Then, after working there for about 5 years, I was just looking for a change. And I saw the MBA as another challenge – that would also stand me in good stead for my career.

Reflecting on all the skills I have gained, I saw that my Masters in Engineering degree had honed my quantitative and analytical skills, and given me a good understanding of operations management, and cultivated an interest too in Technology. I then saw that my experiences at PwC had given me a very good grounding and understanding of Finance & Accounting, and gradually too, better appreciation of some of the risks involved in running a business, from an operational and financial perspective.

Next, from my career, I was looking to further broaden my horizons. Thanks for that Sachin! Well, you definitely seem to be heading in the right direction! I think we’re nearing the end of this talk now. So now, I’d like to ask you two further questions, if you don’t mind. What do you think has made you the person you are today?

What is your definition of wealth and a successful life?

I think there is no clear definition, because it means different things to different people.

  • For some people, financial wealth is very important, not necessarily because they are materialistic, but because it is something their family never had – and this is one way they can achieve that level of comfort.
  • For other people, it might be success with their career
  • For some it might be family with other people.
  • For others, it might be health and fitness.

For me personally, after living in two very small rooms in London, I think my definition of wealth and a successful life, would be some combination of all of the above! You know,… the usual of what men seem to want these days, a large room, with a desk(!), a (solar powered) house, a nice modern (hybrid-electric) sports car, a nice kitchen, some gadgets and electronics, girlfriend/wife, financial security, etc., etc.

I think, if there was to be a very strict definition of wealth and a successful life – it would be an intangible and general one – to just be happy and fulfilled, in whatever it is you do or want to do! But that of course, will mean different things to different people!

If you wake up and feel, “I’m grateful to have this day”, then that is a sign of real success! But if you wake up and think, “I just need to get through this day”, then that is a sign you might want to do something different!

Yes, it’s a very, very small world, and you should never – you must never – burn your bridges, because it will always come back round to you, in the end, at some point in time, or through some channel! All of your actions, and all of your reactions, and the way you treat people, will always come back to you!

You know, that kind of golden rule, “do onto others as you’d like done onto you”. That sort of thing, is something that really resonates with me, and I think, strongly in business, more generally.

Integrity is something you cannot regain!

I’ve seen time and time again, people burn their bridges, push themselves into a corner, and overreact, and overreact badly! (I’ve been guilty of it myself sometimes). But it does no one any favours! So, my one piece of advice would be just that.

Then, my second piece of advice would be to just be true to yourself. Work out what it is that inspires you and gets you out of bed in the morning! What are the one or two things that you really feel passionate about? Then put 110% of yourself behind achieving those things! I know it’s very general, but that’s the best way to achieve your goals.

One last question, and I promise this will be the last. Why did you choose the name Star Tech Enterprises for the venture you currently work through as a freelancer? That’s quite an interesting name!

I wanted name of the company to reflect this in some way. So, I chose “Star”, as I saw that as something that indicated excellence – just like what you see in the night sky, this company too, would try to work with an ambition and standard that is “hotly plasmonic” – believing that every individual, if they truly love their work, should “reach for the Stars!”

Then word “Tech”, I chose because, personally, I believe in the continued application of science and technology, for the benefit of modern society – in making the systems and cultures we live in, more predictable, more effective, and more enjoyable, so that we may all live happier and healthier lives. That is why I wanted to include the word “Tech”.

Then I chose “Enterprises”, because, just like the great explorers of “The Starship Enterprise” from the TV series Star Trek (directed by J.J. Abrams), I wanted the company’s name to reflect the same values of its daring crew of voyagers, namely: “to seek out new discoveries” and “to boldly go where no man or woman has gone before” – always with an “adventurous spirit”, and always with a “high sense of courage, boldness, and ingenuity!”, which is why I chose the word “Enterprises”

Hence, the name “Star-Tech Enterprises”
